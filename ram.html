// COMPONENT 03

RAM.
The Workspace.

Every open tab. Every running game. Every app you're juggling. It all lives in RAM — and when it runs out, everything slows to a crawl.

In plain English: RAM (Random Access Memory) is your computer's short-term memory. It holds everything your CPU is actively working on right now — programs, files, browser tabs. More RAM means more things can stay open and ready at once.

// THE BIG CHOICE

DDR4 vs DDR5.

The generation of RAM you buy is locked to your motherboard and CPU. You can't mix them. Here's what separates them — and which one you should be buying in 2026.

DDR4

// RELEASED 2014 · STILL WIDELY USED

The previous generation — and still a solid choice for budget builds or systems upgrading from an existing DDR4 platform. Slightly slower than DDR5 but more affordable and widely available.

Key specs

  • Speed range — 2133 – 4800 MHz
  • Voltage — 1.2V standard
  • Max per stick — 32 GB
  • Platform — Intel LGA 1700 (some) · AMD AM4

DDR5

// RELEASED 2021 · THE CURRENT STANDARD

The current generation and the right choice for any new build in 2026. Faster bandwidth, higher capacities, and it's the only option for AMD's AM5 platform. Prices have dropped significantly and are now very close to DDR4.

Key specs

  • Speed range — 4800 – 8000+ MHz
  • Voltage — 1.1V standard
  • Max per stick — 64 GB
  • Platform — Intel LGA 1700/1851 · AMD AM5
// THE REAL QUESTION

How much do you need?

More RAM doesn't always mean more performance. The sweet spot depends entirely on what you're doing. Here's a straightforward breakdown.

💾

8 GB

The bare minimum

Enough for basic office work and web browsing — if you keep tabs under control. In 2026, 8 GB will feel tight on modern operating systems alone. Avoid for new builds.

Use case: Budget office PC only

🎮

16 GB

The sweet spot

The standard for gaming and everyday computing in 2026. Handles most modern games, multitasking, and light creative work without breaking a sweat. Recommended for most users.

Use case: Gaming, daily use

🎬

32 GB

The creator's choice

Ideal for video editing, 3D rendering, heavy multitasking, and future-proofing your build. If you stream while gaming or run virtual machines, this is your target. Increasingly the new sweet spot.

Use case: Content creation, streaming

// 2026 SNAPSHOT

Modern standards.

The specs you'll see on every RAM product page — and what they actually mean. Speed, latency, capacity: here's your decoder.

Standard What it means Status (2026)
DDR5-6000 The gaming sweet spot for DDR5. Best balance of speed and latency. MODERN
DDR5-4800 JEDEC baseline for DDR5. Stable and compatible — good budget DDR5 starting point. MODERN
DDR4-3200 The DDR4 sweet spot. Widely compatible, affordable, and still performs well. AGING
XMP / EXPO Intel's XMP and AMD's EXPO are one-click overclocking profiles. Enable in BIOS to reach advertised speeds. MODERN
CL (CAS Latency) Lower CL = faster response. CL30 is excellent for DDR5. Matters less than speed tier for gaming. MODERN
Dual Channel Two sticks instead of one. Doubles memory bandwidth — always run two matched sticks. MODERN
ECC RAM Error-correcting memory for servers and workstations. Not needed for consumer gaming builds. WORKSTATION
// PICK YOUR KIT

Buying guide.

Capacity matters more than speed for most people. Get the right amount first, then worry about frequency. A 32 GB DDR5-4800 kit beats a 16 GB DDR5-6000 kit for productivity every time.

🏷️

Budget Build

~ $40 – $70

16 GB DDR4-3200 (2×8 GB) for an AM4 or older Intel platform. Gets the job done. If you're on a new AM5 board, go 16 GB DDR5-4800 at the same price point.

Pick: 16 GB DDR4-3200 or DDR5-4800

⚡

Gaming Build

~ $80 – $140

16 GB DDR5-6000 (2×8 GB) is the gamer's sweet spot in 2026. Hits the ideal speed for AMD's AM5 Infinity Fabric and Intel's memory controller. Enable XMP/EXPO in BIOS.

Pick: 16 GB DDR5-6000 (2×8 GB)

🖥️

Creator / Pro

~ $150 – $280

32 GB DDR5-6000 (2×16 GB) for video editing, 3D, streaming, and heavy multitasking. Future-proofed for the next 3–4 years. Don't overpay for DDR5-7200+ — the gains are minimal for non-synthetic workloads.

Pick: 32 GB DDR5-6000 (2×16 GB)

Compatibility & dependencies.

RAM doesn't work in isolation. Your motherboard dictates which generation fits, the CPU's memory controller sets the speed ceiling, and the slot count determines if you're running dual channel. Get any of these wrong and your kit won't POST — or won't run at its advertised speed.

⚠️

Your RAM might not run at its advertised speed out of the box

By default, most motherboards boot RAM at the JEDEC baseline speed — DDR5-4800 or DDR4-2133 — regardless of what the kit is rated for. You must go into the BIOS and enable XMP (Intel) or EXPO (AMD) to unlock the advertised speed. This is not overclocking — it's activating a tested and certified profile burned into the RAM stick itself. Skipping this step means you're leaving significant performance on the table without knowing it.

🔧

Single stick = half the bandwidth

Many people build with a single 16 GB stick thinking they can 'add another later.' While true, running in single-channel mode cuts your memory bandwidth in half compared to dual-channel — and bandwidth is what feeds your CPU and integrated GPU. In gaming, this can cost you 10–20% framerate depending on the title and resolution. Always buy in matched pairs: 2×8 GB instead of 1×16 GB, 2×16 GB instead of 1×32 GB. The pair costs the same and performs dramatically better.

⚡
// SPACE-PC GOLDEN TIP

More capacity beats more speed — every time.

The biggest RAM mistake is buying fast, low-capacity RAM when your real bottleneck is running out of memory. DDR5-7200 sounds impressive, but if you're down to 2 GB free while gaming, your OS starts using your SSD as overflow memory (paging) — and that tanks performance by 10× more than any speed upgrade could fix. Rule of thumb: max out your capacity budget first (16 GB minimum, 32 GB if you can), then pick the fastest speed you can afford within that capacity.
