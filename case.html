// COMPONENT 06

PC Case.
The Skeleton.

It holds everything together, dictates your cooling performance, and is the physical face of your entire setup.

In plain English: The case (or chassis) is the metal and glass box that protects your parts. A good case makes building easy, provides space to hide cables, and keeps your components cool by allowing air to flow freely.
Jump to: Airflow vs Glass Sizes Standards Buying Guide Compatibility Golden Tip
// THE BIG CHOICE

Airflow vs Showcase.

The front panel of your case is the most important part of its design. It determines how your PC breathes. You must choose between maximum performance and maximum aesthetics.

Mesh Front (Airflow)

// HIGH PERFORMANCE · COOLER TEMPS

The front panel is a breathable mesh grid. Fans can easily pull fresh, cool air straight into the case to feed the GPU and CPU. Highly recommended for 90% of gaming builds.

Key specs

  • Thermals — Excellent
  • Dust — Trapped by mesh filter
  • Noise — Slightly audible
  • Looks — Aggressive / Functional

Solid / Glass Front

// PREMIUM LOOKS · RESTRICTED AIR

The front panel is solid glass or metal. It looks incredibly sleek, but fans have to struggle to pull air through tiny side vents. Components will run hotter, and fans will spin louder to compensate.

Key specs

  • Thermals — Poor to Average
  • Dust — Builds up in side vents
  • Noise — Louder (fans run at max)
  • Looks — Minimalist / Elegant
// THE FORM FACTOR

What size do you need?

Cases come in three main sizes. The size dictates what motherboards fit, how large your GPU can be, and how much space it takes up on your desk.

🎒

Mini-ITX (SFF)

Small Form Factor

Tiny, portable, and console-like. Looks amazing on a desk, but they are notoriously difficult to build in, require expensive miniature parts (SFX PSUs), and run hotter.

Use case: Portability, Minimalist desk

🏢

Mid-Tower

The Standard

The sweet spot for 95% of PC builders. Fits standard ATX motherboards, supports massive graphics cards, and offers plenty of room for airflow and cable management.

Use case: Standard gaming, First-time builders

🏗️

Full-Tower

The Behemoth

Massive cases meant to sit on the floor. Only necessary if you are running custom liquid cooling loops, multiple GPUs, or E-ATX workstation motherboards. Overkill for regular users.

Use case: Custom loops, Workstations

// 2026 SNAPSHOT

Modern standards.

The specs you'll see on every PC Case product page — and what they actually mean. Clearances, form factors, and I/O.

Standard What it means Status (2026)
GPU Clearance The maximum length of a Graphics Card that will fit. Crucial spec, as modern GPUs (like RTX 4080) are massive. CRITICAL
Radiator Support Indicates if the case can fit a 240mm, 280mm, or 360mm AIO liquid cooler at the top or front. MODERN
Front I/O: Type-C Having a USB-C port on the top/front of the case. A must-have feature for cases in 2026. MODERN
Dual Chamber Cases that look like aquariums (glass on front and side), hiding the power supply behind the motherboard tray. TRENDING
Cable Routing Space The gap behind the motherboard. Look for at least 20mm of space, otherwise the back panel won't close. MODERN
// PICK YOUR SHELL

Buying guide.

A case doesn't directly give you FPS, but it dictates how easy your PC is to build and maintain. Spend the extra $30 for good airflow and included fans.

🏷️

Value Airflow

~ $60 – $90

A standard Mid-Tower with a mesh front panel and 2-3 pre-installed fans. Prioritizes function over form. Gets the job done perfectly for budget and mid-range systems.

Look for: Included fans, Mesh front

⚡

Premium Mid-Tower

~ $100 – $150

The sweet spot. Better build quality, easier cable management channels, USB-C on the front panel, and quieter, high-quality fans included out of the box.

Look for: Type-C, Velcro straps

✨

Showcase / Dual Chamber

~ $160 – $250+

Aquarium-style cases that put your parts on display. They require you to buy 6-10 separate RGB fans to look right and perform well. Purely for high-end aesthetics.

Look for: GPU clearance, Fan capacity

Compatibility & dependencies.

The case is a physical boundary. Every component you buy must mathematically fit inside its dimensions. If you don't check clearances, your side panel won't close.

⚠️

Always check GPU length vs Case clearance

Modern graphics cards like the RTX 4080 are extremely long (often exceeding 330mm). Many older or compact Mid-Towers only support up to 315mm. If your GPU is longer than the case's 'Max GPU Clearance' spec, it physically will not fit. You will have to cut the metal or buy a new case. Check the spec sheets for both products before buying.

🔧

Air cooler height matters

If you are buying a massive Air Tower cooler (like the Noctua NH-D15), it sticks out very far from the motherboard. If your case is too narrow, the glass side panel will hit the cooler and won't close. Check the 'Max CPU Cooler Height' spec on the case and ensure your cooler is shorter than that number.

⚡
// SPACE-PC GOLDEN TIP

Cable management starts with the case.

Building a PC is 30% plugging things in and 70% hiding the cables. A cheap $40 case has zero room behind the motherboard, making cable management a nightmare. A good $90 case includes velcro straps, rubber grommets, and a wide back channel that makes your build look like a professional did it. Save yourself the headache and don't cheap out on the chassis.
